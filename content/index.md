[[Research Questions and Methodology]]

“[[Jank]]” is an often used term in studying and playing games. M.D. Schmalzer's definition of jank is  [[Janky Controls and Embodied Play|"a player’s perception that a videogame does not behave in the ways that it should"]] (Schmalzer, n.d.). Colloquially, the term is used to describe an experience where the outcome of a player’s action in a game does not replicate their expectations. This definition overlaps with common definitions of *glitch*, where a glitch is similarly classified as unexpected behavior of software. Further complicating the definitions of glitch and jank is the notion of software bugs, where [[Debugging game history a critical lexicon|"if... trouble is anticipated, it is thus not classified as a glitch"]] (Guins, 2016).

[[The Game Definition Game|"A game may be an activity confined by rules (Avedon & Sutton-Smith, 1971; Le ́vi-Strauss, 1962/1966; Nachmanovitch, 1990, p. 43), constituted by rules (Suits, 1978), ‘‘a little cosmos of its own’’ brought on by the rules (Riezler, 1941), or a domain of contrived contingency (Malaby, 2007). Some even go so far as to state that a game is its rules (Ellington et al., 1982, p. 9; Parlett, 1999; cf. Hunicke, LeBlanc, & Zubek, 2004)"]] (Stenros, 2017). There are many perspectives on the definition of a game, but for the purposes of this paper the term "game" will be defined as a set of rules within a system followed by one or more players.

The definition of "videogame," however, matters more for the purposes of distinguishing jank from glitch and bug. [[Questions Over Answers Reflective Game Design|"Games are highly related to simulations. Both are often used to model systems, situations and events. Both are essentially sequences of carefully designed experiences, co-created through the decisions of designers and our choices at run-time"]] (Khaled, 2018). A videogame is a system defined by explicit software rules and implicit rules defined by the developer. A software bug occurs as a result of the system *not* working as defined, which is likely due to faulty or incompatible hardware. A glitch occurs as a result of the system working as defined, but not as expected or intended. Jank often occurs as a result of software bugs and glitches, but can also occur during "normal" gameplay, where software/hardware rules allow the rules defined by the developer to be broken and/or defied.

Additionally, there is a social contract that must be formed before an experience in a game can be classified as "jank" instead of as a glitch, as good/bad design, or as simply outdated. This understanding of jank draws many parallels to *reflective game design*, where [[Questions Over Answers Reflective Game Design|"we cannot reflect unless the situation at hand calls to mind other relevant experiences or beliefs; no precedents mean there would be no beliefs to scrutinize in a new light"]] (Khaled, 2018). The greater context of the videogame one is playing as well as games as a whole is often required for one to perceive any given moment in gameplay as "janky." Without the greater context of other videogames, moments where the system extends the player the opportunity to design their own game may be missed or misconstrued as a glitch, as a software bug, or as bad game design. Jank is often best experienced in social settings, as another experienced party is present to confirm if a gameplay moment is janky, or if that moment is intended by either the software or developer.

This is to say that jank is often a spontaneous opportunity for a player to engage in reflective game design. By being pulled out of the gameplay circuit via jank, the player is extended an opportunity to recontextualize the game they are playing within the system. By noting these moments, players are able to further iterate on those games, creating new experiences within systems allowed by but not intended by the system in which they are designing. For instance, YouTuber Dzeeff's Yu-Gi-Oh series *Master Saga* has gone through many ruling iterations as the players became increasingly performant within each ruleset.

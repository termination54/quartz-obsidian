[[Game Ideas]]

“[[Jank]]” is an often used term in studying and playing games. M.D. Schmalzer's definition of jank is "a player’s perception that a videogame does not behave in the ways that it should." Colloquially, the term is used to describe an experience where the outcome of a player’s action in a game does not replicate their expectations. This definition overlaps with common definitions of *glitch*, where a glitch is similarly classified as unexpected behavior of software. Further complicating the definitions of glitch and jank is the notion of software bugs, where "if... trouble is anticipated, it is thus not classified as a glitch."

Jank often occurs as a result of software bugs and glitches, but can also occur during "normal" gameplay. For the purposes of this paper, jank will be used to describe a moment in gameplay where a player is presented the opportunity to design their own game within a system.

There is, undoubtedly, a social contract that must be formed before an experience in a game can be classified as "jank" instead of as a glitch, as good/bad design, or as simply outdated.
